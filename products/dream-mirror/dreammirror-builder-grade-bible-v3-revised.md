1. Product Vision First

DreamMirror is a voice-first, AI-assisted dream reflection platform that helps people turn dreams into emotional insight, recurring pattern recognition, and practical self-understanding. The wedge is simple: when a user wakes up, they can dictate a dream before it fades. The product then structures the narrative, identifies emotional tone, extracts symbolic candidates, connects themes to the user's waking-life context, tracks recurring patterns over time, and offers an optional path to book a human session with Yuni.

The product should not be positioned as fortune telling, supernatural prediction, or a substitute for therapy. The cleanest positioning is: a psychologically grounded dream journal that helps users understand themselves. The app can feel poetic and spiritual, but the core product promise should remain grounded: dreams may reflect emotional patterns, relational dynamics, unresolved stress, inner growth, grief, longing, fear, transition, and identity change. The app helps the user investigate those patterns with curiosity rather than certainty.

The category opportunity is not just "dream interpretation." Generic interpretation is easy to copy. The deeper category is dream intelligence: a personal, longitudinal emotional mirror built from the user's own dream history. The product becomes valuable when it notices that the user is repeatedly dreaming about water, old houses, airports, ex-partners, animals, school, broken rooms, locked doors, vehicles, storms, or family figures, then connects those patterns to the user's evolving life phase. That is the difference between a one-off AI answer and a product users may return to for months.

For Marcus, the key builder directive is this: build the smallest coherent product that proves the behavioral loop. The loop is wake, capture, reflect, save, notice pattern, return. Do not overbuild community, advanced dashboards, native app infrastructure, or complex coaching operations before the loop is validated. The first prototype should make a user feel, within two minutes of waking, "This app understood the emotional shape of my dream better than a generic dream dictionary would have." If that happens repeatedly, there is a real product.

4. Yuni Context for Marcus: Privacy-Safe Method Extraction

This section is intentionally privacy-safe. It gives Marcus the product-relevant context behind Yuni's dream system without exposing private dream details or relational material that would be inappropriate to share with a developer, investor, or external partner. The goal is not to retell Yuni's inner life. The goal is to extract the behavioral method, the recurring analytical structure, and the product opportunity.

Yuni has maintained a recurring dream analysis practice across at least 2025 and 2026, with concentrated, frequent entries in early 2026. From accessible memory and current project context, the observed minimum is 25+ distinct dream-work entries or dream-analysis threads. The actual number is probably higher, but it should not be guessed in the product. Treat the precise corpus count as a required data task: before building any serious symbol-memory layer, export or manually compile the dream corpus and count exact entries by date. The prototype can start with a curated seed corpus of 25 entries. V1 should aim for 50 curated entries. Any marketing claim about "hundreds of dreams" should be avoided unless the corpus is actually counted.

What matters more than the raw count is consistency. Yuni does not merely ask for random interpretations. She repeatedly asks for structured outputs: dream narrative, meaning, subconscious meaning, core meaning, reflection to current life, spiritual poem, symbolic map, emotional pattern closing, and practical integration. Over time, this has become a recognizable system. She often preserves the raw dream first, then asks for a polished dream record, then separates narrative from interpretation. This matters because product users will also need that separation: capture first, interpret second, integrate third.

The strongest observed feature of Yuni's method is that she treats emotional tone as the primary signal. Plot matters, but feeling leads. She often asks what the dream means in relation to her current life, relationships, work transitions, boundaries, identity, confidence, communication, and emotional growth. The dream is rarely interpreted as a fixed external prophecy. It is read as a symbolic reflection of inner process. This is the essential difference between the Yuni Method and a generic symbol dictionary.

Her analysis style is psychologically informed rather than clinically formal. She uses ideas that resemble Jungian symbolic reflection, attachment awareness, nervous-system language, emotional integration, somatic memory, shadow work, narrative psychology, and cognitive reframing. She is not positioned as a licensed clinician. The product should not claim therapy, diagnosis, treatment, or mental health authority. The correct language is "reflective," "psychologically informed," "symbolic," "emotional," and "integrative."

Yuni's recurring output preferences are also product-relevant. She likes clean and spacious sections. She prefers meaning that is alive, not flat. She wants dream work to feel grounded, intimate, soft, and poetic without becoming vague. She often wants a title that carries deep meaning. She values a final poem or closing reflection because it helps transform analysis into integration. For app design, this means the output should not be a dense block of AI text. It should have a clear hierarchy: summary, emotional themes, symbols, life reflection, recurring pattern, reflective question, and optional poetic closing.

Recurring symbol families in the observed corpus include homes, parents' houses, relatives' houses, hotels, villages, schools, shops, roads, vehicles, bridges, water, animals, food, clothing, music, ceremonies, public visibility, old objects, renovated spaces, natural landscapes, intimacy, family figures, romantic figures, work figures, danger, purification, and movement between places. These should not be treated as universal dictionary entries. The product should treat them as symbolic candidates that require emotional context.

The business opportunity is that Yuni already behaves like a founder of a method. The method currently lives in conversation. The app should turn that method into a repeatable flow. The product should not merely "ask AI what a dream means." It should operationalize Yuni's process: record the dream, preserve the user's language, identify emotional tone, map symbols, connect to current life, find recurring themes, and produce a grounded reflection.

6. Product Positioning and Defensibility

DreamMirror should be defended around method and trust, not code. Code can be copied. A generic voice journal can be copied. A prompt that says "interpret this dream" can be copied. The defensibility comes from a coherent methodology, a distinct brand voice, longitudinal emotional memory, and a human founder who can translate the product into sessions, content, workshops, and real-world trust.

The clearest positioning is: a psychologically grounded dream reflection system that helps users notice emotional patterns over time. That language places the app near journaling, coaching, emotional self-awareness, and AI reflection. It avoids the weaker category of novelty dream interpretation.

The product's moat can be built in layers. First is the Yuni Method: emotion-first, symbol-aware, life-connected, agency-returning reflection. Second is longitudinal memory: the system improves because it remembers the user's own recurring symbols and emotional seasons. Third is the human layer: Yuni can offer sessions, interpretations, and content that give the product a face and a philosophy. Fourth is privacy and care: dreams are intimate, and a product that handles them respectfully can create trust.

A strong product sentence for early testing: "DreamMirror helps you understand recurring emotional patterns in your dreams, without turning them into fortune telling." A stronger founder sentence: "Yuni's method blends dream journaling, symbolic interpretation, emotional pattern recognition, and practical life reflection."

7. Competitive Landscape

The dream app space is not empty. That is good news. Existing products prove there is user curiosity around dream capture, interpretation, symbols, and pattern tracking. DreamMirror should not pretend it is the first dream app. It should be clearer about what it does differently.

Dream:ON historically focused on influencing dreams through soundscapes and sleep-timed audio. Somnio presents as a modern dream journal with quick recording, categories, calendar browsing, hashtags, search, export, and a night-friendly UI. Shadow was known as a voice/text dream journal that helped record dreams upon waking and track long-term dream and sleep patterns. Newer AI dream products include apps that offer AI interpretation, psychological perspectives, symbols, emotions, recurring patterns, or multiple interpretation styles. Several apps now claim voice-first capture and AI pattern detection.

DreamMirror therefore cannot compete by saying "we use AI to interpret dreams." That is no longer enough. The differentiator should be: a founder-led methodology, emotionally grounded output, longitudinal personal symbolism, optional human sessions, and a premium brand that avoids both shallow mysticism and sterile journaling.

9. V1 to V3 Roadmap

V1 should deepen the loop after V0 proves capture and interpretation. Add recurring symbol memory, better dashboarding, weekly summaries, exportable PDF/email reports, and a more polished session booking flow. V1 should not yet become a full social network or coach marketplace.

V2 can introduce paid subscription layers: advanced pattern recognition, monthly reports, deeper personalization, guided dream recall prompts, and curated "Yuni Method" reflections. This is where the app becomes more than a journal.

V3 is the platform layer: practitioner tools, retreat partnerships, multilingual support, optional therapist-compatible exports, anonymized aggregate research, and potentially a native app. V3 only makes sense if V0/V1 retention proves the core behavior.

V0 question: Will people capture dreams and feel understood?

V1 question: Will people return because patterns are visible?

V2 question: Will people pay for deeper insight and reports?

V3 question: Can the method scale into an ecosystem without losing trust?

10. AI Pipeline and Prompt Orchestration

The AI system should not be one giant prompt. It should behave like a compact pipeline of specialized reasoning steps. The user should see one coherent analysis, but the builder can structure the backend as separate prompt stages or internal modules.

Recommended pipeline: ingestion, cleanup, narrative reconstruction, entity extraction, emotion analysis, symbol interpretation candidates, personal context retrieval, pattern comparison, synthesis, safety review, and final formatting. In V0, some of these can be collapsed. The important thing is to preserve the logic.

11. Worked Prompt Example

The prompt example below uses dummy content. It is intentionally not based on Yuni's private dreams. It shows how prior context, emotional check-in, and dream text should be passed into the model.

Example Input Payload

user_profile: User is in a career and relationship transition. User prefers grounded, reflective interpretation, not mystical certainty.

recent_patterns: water appeared in 3 of the last 5 dreams; travel appeared in 2; locked or blocked spaces appeared in 2.

emotional_checkin: User woke feeling confused but strangely calm. Intensity 6/10. User says current life also feels like waiting for direction.

raw_dream: I was in an old hotel near the ocean. I had to catch a ferry but could not find the ticket. I found a blue locked door. Behind it I could hear music, but no one opened it. I felt nervous, then peaceful.

Example System Direction

You are DreamMirror, a psychologically grounded dream reflection assistant. Do not predict the future. Do not diagnose. Interpret dreams as symbolic emotional material. Prioritize emotional tone, user context, and recurring patterns. Use uncertainty language. Return agency to the user. Output sections: Dream Summary, Emotional Themes, Symbol Map, Reflection to Life, Recurring Pattern, Question for Reflection, Gentle Closing.

Example Output Target

Dream Summary: You are in an old hotel near the ocean, trying to catch a ferry but missing the ticket. A locked blue door holds music behind it. The dream moves from nervousness to peace.

Emotional Themes: The emotional arc suggests uncertainty around movement, timing, and access. The calm ending matters: something in you may be learning to stay steady even when the next door is not open yet.

Symbol Map: The ocean may point to emotional depth or transition. The ferry may represent passage into a new phase. The missing ticket may reflect anxiety about readiness or permission. The locked blue door may symbolize a part of your inner life that feels close but not yet accessible. Music behind the door suggests possibility, creativity, or emotional resonance waiting to be approached.

Reflection to Life: Since you noted that waking life feels like waiting for direction, this dream may be mirroring a transition where you sense movement ahead but do not yet feel fully prepared.

Recurring Pattern: Water and travel have appeared recently, which may indicate an ongoing emotional transition rather than a one-time dream theme.

Question for Reflection: Where in your life are you waiting for permission when you may actually need preparation, patience, or a gentler doorway?

Gentle Closing: This dream does not say the door will open. It suggests you can listen without forcing it.

12. Data Model and Memory Architecture

The memory layer is the future moat. V0 can start with a simple database, but the schema should anticipate longitudinal pattern analysis. At minimum, each dream should store raw text, clean narrative, date, time, emotions, intensity, symbols, people, places, themes, life context, analysis output, user edits, and whether the user found it accurate.

A simple schema can include Users, Dreams, DreamSymbols, DreamEmotions, Analyses, Reflections, Sessions, and UserPatternSummaries. Do not over-engineer the graph layer in V0. Instead, store enough structured metadata that V1 can build dashboards from it.

Dream object: id, user_id, timestamp, raw_text, clean_text, title, source_type, privacy_level.

Emotion object: dream_id, emotion_label, intensity, waking_feeling, user_confirmed.

Symbol object: dream_id, symbol_label, category, possible_meanings, confidence, user_confirmed.

Analysis object: dream_id, model_version, prompt_version, summary, output_json, safety_flags.

Pattern summary: user_id, period, recurring_symbols, recurring_emotions, recurring_life_themes, generated_at.

13. Technical Constraints and Build Preferences

The stack is intentionally not mandated. Marcus should choose whatever enables fast iteration. The preferred deployment shape for V0 is a responsive web app or PWA, not native iOS/Android. Native can come later after retention data.

Constraints and preferences: keep the app cheap to run, reduce unnecessary model calls, store structured analysis outputs, and avoid sending sensitive dream text to tools that are not needed. Use a cheaper model or deterministic logic for extraction where possible, and reserve stronger model calls for synthesis. Cache analysis results. Summarize long-term memory rather than sending every prior dream into every prompt. Give users export and delete controls from day one.

Budget target for V0: keep infra plus model costs low enough that 50 beta users are inexpensive to support. The practical target is to avoid any architecture where every dream triggers multiple expensive model calls without caching or summarization. If one dream uses multiple internal prompts, log token cost per stage.

15. Coaching Layer and Human-in-the-Loop Design

The coaching layer is a major differentiator. Most dream apps end at content. DreamMirror can route the user from insight to a human conversation. However, coaching must remain cleanly defined. Yuni should not be positioned as a therapist unless she becomes licensed. The offer is dream reflection, emotional pattern interpretation, and life integration.

A standard session can be 60 to 90 minutes. The user submits one to three dreams in advance. Yuni reviews emotional themes, symbol patterns, and current-life reflections. The output is a written summary, key symbols, one integration question, and optional follow-up practice. The app can generate a pre-session brief for Yuni, but Yuni should always own the final human interpretation.

16. Legal, Safety, and Privacy Guidance for V0

V0 needs legal and safety language before real users test it, because dream content can contain sensitive emotional, relational, sexual, family, health, spiritual, or traumatic material. This does not require a perfect legal department on day one, but it does require clear disclaimers, basic terms, privacy policy, and user control.

Minimum disclaimer copy for V0: "DreamMirror provides reflective dream journaling and AI-assisted interpretation for personal insight. It is not medical advice, mental health treatment, therapy, diagnosis, crisis support, or fortune telling. Interpretations are possibilities, not facts. If you are in crisis or need professional support, contact a qualified professional or local emergency service."

Minimum privacy commitments: users own their dream entries; users can delete entries; users can export entries; dream content is not shared publicly without explicit consent; dream content is not used in marketing without written permission; sensitive data should not be used for model training unless a user explicitly opts in.

Minimum terms guidance: age limit, acceptable use, no crisis support, no guarantee of accuracy, limitation of liability, user responsibility for decisions, and a statement that AI outputs can be incomplete or mistaken.

17. Brand Voice: Good Voice and Bad Voice

The brand voice should be warm, grounded, psychologically intelligent, and lightly poetic. It should not sound like a guru, fortune teller, chatbot therapist, or generic wellness influencer.

Good voice: "This dream may be showing you the emotional shape of a transition, not giving you a final answer."

Good voice: "The locked door feels less like rejection and more like timing: something is near, but not yet ready to open."

Good voice: "Your dream returns you to the same theme: movement, uncertainty, and the need to trust your own pace."

Bad voice: "This dream means your soulmate is coming soon."

Bad voice: "You have unresolved trauma and need to confront it immediately."

Bad voice: "The universe is sending you a coded message that you must obey."

Bad voice: "This symbol always means betrayal."

Bad voice: "Your subconscious is proving that this person is bad for you."

Bad voice: "Here is the scientific diagnosis of your dream."

The voice rule is: offer meaning without stealing agency. The user should feel seen, not controlled.

18. Go-to-Market: Ubud Pilot to Global Audience

Ubud is a natural pilot market because the density of seekers, travelers, healers, retreat visitors, coaches, and people in transition is unusually high. The first users do not need to come from paid ads. They can come from real conversations, cafe demos, QR cards, small dream circles, Instagram storytelling, and referral-based sessions.

The offline pitch should be simple: "I am testing a dream reflection app that helps you understand recurring emotional patterns in your dreams. Want to try it?" The QR card should lead to the web app or a landing page. The early CTA should be either "record your dream" or "book a beta session."

Content should demonstrate the method without giving away private client material. Strong content formats: anonymous symbol breakdowns, "what water dreams can reflect," "why houses show up in dreams," "dreams during transition," "how to record a dream before it fades," and Yuni's own founder story in privacy-safe form. Avoid guru claims. Avoid overpromising. The brand grows through trust.

19. Founder Operating System and Data Collection Tasks

Before building advanced personalization, complete the data collection task. Export or manually compile Yuni's dream corpus into a structured table. Fields should include date, raw dream, polished narrative, emotional tone, symbols, people, places, life reflection, output format, poem included, and post-dream real-life connection. Count exact entries. Do not rely on memory estimates.

Year-one target metrics should be modest and real: 100 beta users, 30 users with three or more dreams, 20 paying app subscribers, 10 paid sessions, 5 testimonials, and one clearly documented method manual. The objective is not vanity scale. The objective is proof that the method helps people and that the product loop creates return behavior.

Weekly founder rhythm: analyze dreams, talk to users, document method, publish two to three pieces of content, run one to three sessions, review product feedback, refine prompts, and update the symbol ontology. Marcus can build quickly, but the learning loop depends on Yuni's direct user contact.
