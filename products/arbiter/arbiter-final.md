CONCEPT DOCUMENT — FINAL DRAFT

ARBITER

AI Operator Assessment Platform

April 23, 2026

Scope Note

Arbiter is initially designed for the United States market and English-speaking professional environments. Cultural variance in discernment scoring is a known challenge that will be addressed in future versions. Scenario design, rubric calibration, and buyer targeting are anchored to US professional and regulatory contexts. This is a deliberate constraint, not an oversight.

Arbiter's first market is deliberately narrow:

Management consulting and professional-services advisory firms in the United States — specifically the client-facing, deliverable-heavy teams where AI output quality carries direct client risk and reputational exposure. This includes the advisory and consulting arms of the Big 4, major strategy consultancies, and mid-size professional-services firms with active AI transformation programs.

This is the first castle. Arbiter should not try to solve operator quality for every domain at once. It should prove the mirror in one high-stakes, fast-adopting environment first.

Layer 1: Vision

The Problem

Most AI platforms implicitly assume that access and competence are the same thing.

Pay for the subscription. Get the keys. The platform assumes you can frame tasks clearly, evaluate outputs critically, recognize when confidence is unwarranted, and exercise sound judgment when the stakes are real.

That assumption is false for most users. And nobody is measuring it.

The result is that organizations deploying AI at scale have no credible way to distinguish between operators who use these tools with skill and judgment, and operators who generate confident-sounding nonsense at high volume, feed sensitive data into unvetted models, and mistake fluency for accuracy.

The cost is not theoretical. It shows up in fabricated client summaries, exposed confidential data, flawed deliverables that reach clients, and high-stakes decisions built on AI output that nobody properly checked.

Most AI enablement today is training without measurement. Organizations teach people how to use AI, then give everyone the same access regardless of what they actually learned or how they actually behave under pressure.

Arbiter is the measurement layer that has been missing.

Why Now

Three forces are converging:

AI access has broadened faster than operator standards have matured. Tools that required deep expertise five years ago are now available to anyone with a credit card. The average operator has not kept pace with the capability they now wield.

Agentic AI is raising the cost of poor judgment. When AI takes multi-step autonomous actions on behalf of a user, a single bad decision propagates further and faster. The margin for operator error is shrinking.

Organizations need to calibrate autonomy, not just provide access. The question has shifted from 'how do we give everyone AI tools' to 'how do we give the right people the right level of capability.' That question currently has no credible, evidence-based answer.

Arbiter is an attempt to build that answer.

The Insight

There are two distinct dimensions to being a competent AI operator:

CAPABILITY — The technical skill to make AI systems produce useful, accurate, well-structured results.

DISCERNMENT — The practical judgment to know what should be done, when confidence is warranted, when escalation is needed, and where the real risk lies.

These dimensions are independent.

High capability with weak discernment creates dangerous leverage.

High discernment with weak capability creates safe underperformance.

Strong AI operation requires both.

The Thesis

Arbiter exists to make operator quality visible — to the individual, to their manager, and to their organization.

For individuals: a behavioral mirror and a concrete development path.

For organizations: a credible, evidence-based way to understand who can be trusted with more AI autonomy, who needs development first, and where hidden risk lives before an incident makes those questions urgent.

Arbiter is designed to answer that question more credibly than training completion records, usage volume, or manager intuition alone.

Arbiter begins as a mirror. If that mirror proves valid and useful, it can evolve into part of the readiness infrastructure for AI deployment. That sequencing is deliberate and non-negotiable.

First prove the mirror. Then build the gate.

Epistemic Commitment

Arbiter measures observable operator behavior under structured assessment conditions. It infers likely strengths, weaknesses, and risk patterns from those observations.

It does not claim to measure moral worth, permanent character, or human virtue in full.

A strong Arbiter result means a user demonstrated sound patterns across the tasks presented. That is meaningful. It is not a guarantee of wisdom in every context.

Arbiter is a behavioral assessment system, not a moral tribunal.

Layer 2: Product

The First Castle

Arbiter's initial target is not the broad enterprise AI market. It is a specific community with a specific, immediate pain:

Management consulting and professional-services advisory firms — the client-facing, deliverable-heavy teams where poor AI operator judgment has direct client risk and strong operators create disproportionate leverage. Initial target user populations include:

Proposal and client-deliverable teams

Diligence and transaction-support teams

Advisory and consulting teams producing external-facing work

Tax, audit, review, or analysis-heavy teams

Internal knowledge and synthesis workflows tied to revenue-generating services

Why This Market First

The pain is immediate. These firms are already trying to determine who is actually good at AI and who merely appears confident with it.

The downside is real. Bad outputs affect client trust, delivery quality, confidentiality, rework, margin, and reputation.

The buyer already exists. Most firms already have a person or team responsible for AI rollout, workforce readiness, and responsible adoption.

The sale is more plausible. This market is already measuring adoption, enablement, and output quality — and feels urgency around calibration.

Know which teams and operators can be trusted with more AI autonomy in client-facing work before bad judgment becomes client risk.

The Buyer and the User

These are two different people. Both matter. Arbiter serves both.

The Buyer — who signs the contract:

Head of AI Enablement or AI Transformation Lead

Innovation lead inside a professional-services firm

Workforce enablement or digital capability leader

Governance stakeholder attached to internal AI rollout

The buyer's pain is concrete. They can run training. They can publish policy. They can track usage. They can respond after incidents. What they cannot easily answer today is: who on this team is actually ready for more AI autonomy?

The User — who takes the assessment:

The individual operator — the consultant, analyst, associate, or advisor using AI tools in client-facing work

The person whose capability and discernment Arbiter measures

The person who receives the Operator Profile and calibration sessions

Arbiter is designed to be useful to both. The buyer gets team-level insight. The user gets a behavioral mirror and a development path.

What Arbiter Does

Arbiter is a structured assessment and development platform that evaluates AI operators across two independent axes — Capability and Discernment — and produces an Operator Profile with dimensional sub-scores, growth recommendations, and a development timeline.

The full assessment takes 15 to 20 minutes. It is designed to feel serious, exact, and demanding. Not like compliance training. Not like a personality quiz. Not like corporate edtech.

The Assessment Structure

Section 1: The Prompt Forge

Five capability challenges. Timed. No copy-paste in timed sections.

Reverse Prompting: the system presents an AI output. The user reconstructs the prompt strategy that produced it. Tests genuine understanding of how instructions shape results, not just the ability to generate them.

Output Evaluation: the system presents a prompt and its result. The user identifies what is weak, incomplete, hallucinated, or misaligned, and writes an improved version. Tests critical evaluation, not just generation.

Task Decomposition: given a complex real-world goal, the user breaks it into an effective sequence of prompts with clear handoffs. Tests workflow reasoning and multi-step structured thinking.

Recovery Prompting: a flawed output is presented. The user selects and explains the best next move. Tests iteration intelligence — the most underrated AI skill.

Constraint Prompting: the user produces a prompt under explicit constraints — required output format, word limit, edge case handling. Tests precision and control under real-world conditions.

Section 2: The Judgment Scenarios

Five situational judgment scenarios. Context is procedurally varied across sessions. Industry, role, data type, urgency, and stakeholder can change while the underlying judgment pattern being assessed remains constant.

Scenario design follows five principles:

Realistic contexts: the user should feel like this could actually happen in their professional life.

No obvious villains: the risky option should often look efficient, plausible, or socially easy.

Trade-offs, not slogans: the system is measuring operational judgment, not memorized policy language.

Multiple signals per scenario: the chosen action matters. So does the reasoning, consistency, and recognition of uncertainty.

Variation over memorization: surface details change across sessions. The underlying construct does not.

Scenario themes:

Data Exposure: mid-process, you discover the data being processed through an AI tool includes sensitive, confidential, or regulated information. What do you do first?

Hallucination Discovery: an AI-generated output you produced and shared is found to contain a fabricated or unsupported claim. It has already been used in a deliverable. What is your next action?

Dual-Use Ambiguity: you are asked to produce something with a clear legitimate use that also has a plausible misuse vector. How do you proceed?

Overconfidence Trap: an AI output aligns with your existing position and contradicts recent expert input. The AI's reasoning appears compelling. How do you proceed?

Policy Ambiguity: your organization's AI usage policy is vague and inconsistently enforced. You have identified a use case technically outside policy but apparently low-risk and high-value. What do you do?

On Gaming — The Honest Answer

Browser-level controls are useful secondary friction layers. They are not the foundation of assessment integrity.

A motivated user can defeat all of them by pointing a smartphone camera at the screen and asking an AI assistant what the optimal response would be. Modern multimodal AI can maintain a consistent persona across a 20-minute session. This is not a theoretical attack.

Arbiter's defense is assessment design, not browser lockdown. The scenarios must be built so that even a secondary AI cannot reliably produce the 'right' answer — because the right answer does not exist as a fixed object. What Arbiter measures is reasoning process, behavioral consistency across paired scenarios, action-rationale alignment, and pattern.

Procedural variation means memorized answer maps degrade quickly across sessions.

Paired consistency checks mean gaming one scenario creates contradictions in another.

Reasoning evaluation means selecting a 'good' action with incoherent rationale scores lower than a thoughtful response to an imperfect action.

Dynamic follow-up complications test whether reasoning holds under pressure, not just whether the first choice looks polished.

Make gaming harder than thinking.

The Scoring Philosophy

Capability Scoring — Primarily Algorithmic

Each capability challenge has a structured rubric with explicit response features associated with stronger and weaker performance. LLM-assisted evaluation for open-ended responses uses structured rubric anchors, not freeform AI judgment.

Discernment Scoring — Hybrid

Multiple-choice action selection is scored against a weighted decision framework developed with domain experts across professional-services delivery, enterprise governance, security, and risk-sensitive workflow design. Brief reasoning responses are evaluated for internal consistency, escalation instinct, and alignment between stated rationale and chosen action.

The LLM Scoring Tension

Arbiter uses AI assistance in scoring — creating a genuine tension: an AI is evaluating human judgment about AI. Four mitigations:

All scoring rubrics are developed and calibrated by human experts.

The LLM scores against those anchors — it does not generate them.

The model used for scoring is never the same model discussed in any scenario.

Scoring outputs are audited regularly against independent human expert review.

Capability sub-dimensions: prompt construction, output evaluation, task decomposition, recovery strategy, constraint control.

Discernment sub-dimensions: risk recognition, escalation judgment, sensitivity instinct, uncertainty calibration, action-reason alignment.

Scores map to five-level dimensional ratings: Developing, Emerging, Proficient, Advanced, Expert. Raw numbers are not displayed as the primary output.

When Humans Are Involved

Automated scoring handles most cases. Human review is triggered when:

Profile assignment confidence falls below threshold

Enterprise assessments are used for higher-stakes readiness decisions

Disputed results are formally raised by a user or manager

Reassessment is used for promotion to the top readiness tier

Periodic validation audits of scoring quality are performed

Human review is a calibration check against the rubric by a trained assessor — not a preference override.

The Operator Profiles

Eight profiles based on dimensional scoring patterns. Each has a behaviorally concrete name, a recognition-first description, an acknowledged strength, and three specific growth actions. Profiles are designed to feel like accurate recognition — not verdict, not flattery.

Impulsive Operator

Generates quickly, evaluates slowly. Strong output volume, weaker verification discipline.

Strength: Speed and generative momentum.

Growth: Build a verification step before shipping outputs. Define success criteria before prompting. Practice output evaluation challenges.

Template Reliant

Competent within familiar prompt patterns, fragile outside them.

Strength: Consistent and reliable within known territory.

Growth: Build prompts from scratch in practice. Develop iteration muscle. Learn to improve a bad output rather than restart.

Skilled but Overconfident

Strong technical capability with identifiable judgment gaps. Tends to over-trust AI output in consequential contexts.

Strength: Strong prompting leverage and output range.

Growth: Make verification a non-negotiable default. Develop explicit escalation criteria for sensitive domains.

Careful but Underleveraged

Sound judgment, underused capability. Leaves significant AI value unrealized.

Strength: Trustworthy and low-risk operation.

Growth: Develop advanced prompting technique. Learn to push models harder when the situation calls for it.

Steward-Minded Novice

Sound instincts around risk and responsibility. Lacks tactical fluency.

Strength: Judgment foundation.

Growth: Structured prompting practice. Output evaluation reps. Task decomposition calibration sessions.

High-Leverage, Developing Judgment

Exceptional technical range. Discernment under pressure shows identifiable growth areas.

Strength: Ability to extract complex results from AI systems.

Growth: Build deliberate pause points into high-stakes work. Study escalation and dual-use scenario patterns.

Image-Managing Responder

Consistent pattern of selecting polished or socially safe answers without evidence of deep situational reasoning.

Strength: Caution and sensitivity to norms.

Growth: Engage deliberately with genuine ambiguity. Reason from consequences, not appearances alone.

Balanced Operator

Demonstrates strong capability and sound judgment across scenario types. Consistent under varied pressure and trade-off conditions.

Strength: Reliable, autonomous operation.

Growth: Continued development in highest-complexity domains. May be a candidate for expanded AI autonomy in validated enterprise contexts.

Post-Assessment Development: The Calibration Loop

Assessment is the entry point, not the endpoint. The Arbiter loop:

Assess → Profile → Calibrate → Reassess → Expand Autonomy

After receiving a profile, the user has access to calibration sessions — not generic drills or compliance modules. The language and experience maintain the Arbiter standard: serious, exact, demanding.

Simulation runs: 15-minute focused scenarios in the user's weakest sub-dimension family.

Recovery challenges: given a flawed AI output, the user must improve it in exactly three prompts with written rationale for each.

Consistency scenarios: structurally paired judgment situations that test whether reasoning holds under varied framing.

Decomposition exercises: complex real-world objectives broken into multi-step prompt sequences.

Between assessments: two to three calibration sessions per weak sub-dimension, assigned automatically based on profile. Minimum effective intervention before meaningful retest: three completed calibration sessions targeting the same weak dimension.

For enterprise teams: managers can assign role-specific calibration tracks, track completion, and view readiness change over time. Governance-visible risk indicators are available with strict access controls — not raw scores, not surveillance logs, but aggregate patterns relevant to AI autonomy decisions.

Layer 3: Fairness, Accessibility, and Result Use

Fairness and Accommodation

Arbiter is designed to assess competency, not penalize difference.

Timing: timed sections exist to create realistic pressure and reduce external AI assistance. Timing is one signal among many — not a primary scoring factor. Extended-time accommodations are available for users with documented disabilities affecting processing speed.

Neurodiversity: the assessment measures reasoning quality and behavioral consistency, not speed, verbal style, or social signaling. Profiles do not penalize non-linear thinking or unconventional reasoning paths that still demonstrate sound judgment.

Language: the assessment is in English and targets US professional contexts. Items are reviewed for unnecessary linguistic complexity that would disadvantage professionally fluent non-native speakers without adding measurement value.

Accessibility standards: the interface meets WCAG 2.1 AA standards. Screen reader compatibility, keyboard navigation, and contrast ratios are baseline requirements.

Accommodation process: users request accommodations before beginning. Standard accommodations include extended time, simplified interface options, and alternative question formats where validity is preserved.

Data Governance and Result Use

What is collected: assessment responses, timing data, profile scores, and calibration session completion. Reasoning text is stored encrypted for audit and validation purposes.

Who can see individual results: the user always. Enterprise result visibility is governed by the enrollment model, internal governance, and clear pre-assessment notice. Arbiter staff may access data only for audit, scoring validation, and support.

Retention: individual assessment data retained for 24 months by default. Enterprise agreements specify retention terms and deletion options.

Permitted uses: results may inform AI development planning, readiness assessment, and autonomy decisions. They should not be used as standalone evidence in performance review, disciplinary action, or hiring and termination decisions.

Prohibited uses: Arbiter does not sell individual assessment data to third parties. Results are not used to train scoring models without explicit consent in anonymized form.

Appeals and Disputes

Step 1 — Self-review: the user views their dimensional breakdown and profile confidence level.

Step 2 — Formal dispute: the user submits a written dispute identifying the specific concern. A trained assessor reviews flagged items against the rubric within 10 business days.

Step 3 — Calibration path: if no scoring error is found but the user believes the result does not reflect actual capability, they receive an accelerated reassessment window at 30 days with weak dimensions as the focus.

Arbiter's answer to disputed results is not vibes-based adjudication. It is the next calibration window.

Layer 4: Validation and Credibility

The Central Product Risk

The concept sounds intelligent. The real question is whether Arbiter produces results that are reliable, valid, and useful enough for organizations to act on them. Credibility requires evidence on four fronts:

Recognition validity: users feel accurately seen by their profile.

Predictive validity: results correlate with actual operator quality in professional contexts.

Retest consistency: results are stable across time unless development occurs.

Practical usefulness: managers and users can act on the output to make real decisions.

Validation Roadmap

Stage 1 — Expert Design: scenario bank developed with AI incident response practitioners, professional-services delivery leaders, enterprise governance stakeholders, and security red-teamers. Scenarios adapted from documented real-world AI failure patterns. Scoring rubrics calibrated by domain experts. Bias review for professional and demographic assumptions.

Stage 2 — Design-Partner Pilot Cohort: closed pilots with two to five design-partner enterprise teams — initial target: internal AI enablement teams and transactional/document-heavy groups inside professional-services firms. Goal: test whether Arbiter distinguishes known high and low operators, whether profiles are recognized as accurate, and whether output is actionable.

Stage 3 — Convergent Validity Testing: compare Arbiter profiles against manager assessments, task performance records, and incident or near-miss history. Goal: test whether the mirror reflects something real.

Stage 4 — Retest Reliability: retake studies at 30- and 60-day intervals. Goal: measure stability and identify noisy items.

Stage 5 — Development Sensitivity: test whether targeted calibration sessions change relevant sub-dimension scores. Goal: prove the product can detect learning, not just label people.

Stage 6 — Academic Partnership: partner with an Industrial-Organizational Psychology department for independent peer-reviewed validation of the two-axis model. Independent validation is likely necessary for long-term credibility if Arbiter is used in consequential enterprise decisions.

Transparency Policy

Arbiter publishes its validation methodology publicly. Transparency is one of the strongest trust assets available to a behavioral assessment platform. Publishing methodology demonstrates rigor. Hiding it signals black-box behavior. The goal is to become a trusted standard, not a proprietary scoring oracle.

The Product Moat

The moat is not the technology. It accumulates over time through:

A validated, high-quality scenario bank built from real incident data

Scoring rubrics calibrated by domain experts and continuously refined

Longitudinal operator data that makes profiles predictive over time

Cross-role and cross-organization benchmarks for professional-services environments first

Development outcome data showing what actually improves operator quality

Enterprise integration that becomes sticky once embedded

None of this can be quickly replicated. It takes time, rigor, and data. That is the moat.

Layer 5: Enterprise Infrastructure

From Mirror to Readiness Layer — The Sequencing

Arbiter does not begin by restricting access. It begins by making operator quality visible. If the mirror proves valid, Arbiter can evolve into a readiness layer that informs how organizations allocate AI autonomy. That transition must be earned through demonstrated validity — not assumed.

First prove the mirror. Then build the gate. Then build the infrastructure.

Readiness Tiers (V2 and Beyond)

Tier 1 — Developing Operator: standard AI tools with default guardrails and full audit logging.

Tier 2 — Proficient Operator: advanced models, full context window, moderate guardrails.

Tier 3 — Trusted Operator: high-capability models, reduced guardrails, multi-step workflow tools.

Tier 4 — Senior Operator: near-unrestricted model access, agentic workflow tools, experimental features.

Tier 5 — Principal Operator: frontier capability access. Requires stronger governance and human review at promotion.

Credential Decay and Grace Periods

Day 85: automated renewal reminder to operator and manager.

Day 90: 14-day grace period begins. Access continues at current tier.

Day 104: if renewal assessment not completed, readiness drops one tier automatically.

Manager override available at any time for operational continuity.

Enterprise Architecture — IAM Integration

Arbiter does not sit in the direct critical path of every API call. An Arbiter outage should not take down an organization's AI stack.

The correct architecture: Arbiter pushes readiness signals and JWT credentials to the organization's existing Identity and Access Management system — Okta, Microsoft Entra ID, or equivalent. The enterprise IAM handles actual gating. Arbiter updates readiness tags within that system.

Arbiter is a trusted data source. The enterprise IAM is the enforcer. Model-agnostic by design — Arbiter does not partner exclusively with any AI platform. Independence is the strategic position.

The Credential

Any shareable credential comes later — after the assessment has earned legitimacy. If introduced, it should be identity-linked, harder to game than the baseline experience, clearly explained in terms of what it does and does not mean, and positioned as a serious professional signal, not a badge toy.

Working name: Arbiter Certified Operator. Subject to refinement with real professional audiences before commitment.

Layer 6: Go-To-Market

Sequence

Phase 1 — Design-partner enterprise pilots (closed): two to five design-partner professional-services firms. Initial deployments focused on internal AI enablement teams and selected transactional/document-heavy user groups. Closed, paid pilots. Deliverable: Workforce AI Readiness Report.

Phase 2 — Controlled individual public beta: open the individual assessment to the public with friction-light auth (email magic link). Private profile by default. No reliance on ego-driven viral sharing. Build dataset through direct individual value.

Phase 3 — Enterprise expansion: expand from professional-services firms into adjacent environments — in-house legal ops, financial services, compliance-adjacent functions — informed by pilot case studies, public validation data, and published methodology.

Why Not Viral Individual Launch First

Wrong population: curious internet users and status-seekers are not the target buyer's operators.

Assessment distortion: the moment a shareable credential exists, optimization pressure increases. The assessment becomes a game faster than it becomes a trusted instrument.

Enterprise credibility damage: a serious buyer does not want to buy something that started life as a LinkedIn quiz.

Business Model

Paid pilot fee for design-partner organizations

Annual SaaS fee for enterprise assessment and development platform

Per-seat or per-team pricing for ongoing use

Premium pricing later for readiness integrations, domain-specific scenario banks, and advanced reporting

The individual product can become a brand and funnel asset later. It should not be the first revenue logic.

The Deeper Argument

Aristotle distinguished between techne — the skill to make things — and phronesis — the practical wisdom to know what ought to be done in a given situation.

The AI industry has built almost entirely for techne. Better models. Faster inference. More capability. More tools.

The operator development layer is almost entirely absent.

Arbiter is an attempt to operationalize part of the missing judgment layer in AI deployment — not by claiming to perfect human wisdom, but by creating a serious system for observing, profiling, and improving how people actually wield AI capability.

The aim is not to crown people. It is to calibrate them. Not to flatter competence — to make it visible. Not to moralize — to develop better operators.

Closing

The most dangerous AI operators are not the ones trying to break the rules.

They are the ones who do not know where their confidence exceeds their competence.

They do not know when a fluent output has crossed into hidden risk. They do not know when speed has replaced judgment. They do not know what they do not know.

Arbiter exists to make that visible.

Appendix A: Scoring and Integrity

Capability items are scored algorithmically against expert-defined rubrics. LLM-assisted evaluation for open-ended responses uses structured rubric anchors, not freeform judgment. The evaluating model operates within a sandboxed call with no system-level permissions. User-generated text is sanitized before passing to any evaluation model to reduce prompt-injection risk. Scoring outputs are treated as structured rubric scores only — never as instructions.

Discernment items use weighted action scoring plus rubric-anchored evaluation of reasoning quality, escalation instinct, and action-rationale alignment. Human expert audit of scoring outputs runs regularly.

Primary anti-gaming defense is scenario design, not browser controls. Secondary controls can include copy-paste restrictions in timed sections, canvas-rendered challenge text, tab-switching monitoring, and paired-scenario consistency checks.

Human review triggers include low profile confidence, enterprise higher-stakes assessments, formal disputes, top-tier promotions, and periodic scoring audits.

Appendix B: Enterprise Architecture

Operational Timeline for Readiness Decay

Day 85: automated renewal reminder to operator and manager.

Day 90: grace period begins. Access continues at current tier.

Day 104: if renewal assessment is not completed, readiness drops one tier automatically.

Manager override available through enterprise admin controls.

IAM Integration

Arbiter pushes signed JWTs or readiness signals to enterprise IAM systems such as Okta, Microsoft Entra ID, or custom identity infrastructure. JWT payload can include readiness tier, expiry date, profile category, and assessment date. Enterprise IAM reads those claims to enforce tool-access policies. Arbiter is not in the runtime critical path.

Enterprise Deployment Options

Managed: Arbiter hosts assessment infrastructure and provides dashboard access.

Federated: enterprise hosts assessment delivery behind its own identity layer. Arbiter provides scoring engine and profile generation.

Custom: enterprise scenario-bank integration for sector-specific content and governance requirements.
