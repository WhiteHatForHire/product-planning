The Thing I Would Actually Do Next

Claude is right about this too: stop polishing after one more v5 pass.

Then run a three-hour fake-intake simulation.

Use 5 fake customers:

jet-lagged villa guest

anxious overstimulated traveler

post-yoga sore nomad

couple wanting in-villa massage

spiritually curious retreat participant

Create 10 fake provider profiles.

Run the matching engine manually or through Claude.

Ask:

Did the AI pick the right provider?

Did the explanation feel persuasive?

Did any safety issue appear?

Was the match obvious without AI?

What provider data was missing?

What intake question was annoying?

What would make someone book?

That will teach more than another 20 pages of vision.

Final Man-to-Man Take

This is now good. Really good. But the next level is less fantasy, more falsifiability.

The idea investors would like is not “AI massage app in Bali.”

The idea is:

A state-based matching layer for wellness and local experience economies, beginning with a high-intent, trust-sensitive, same-day use case in Bali.

The reason it is compelling is:

huge wellness/tourism demand

broken local discovery

fragmented provider supply

AI-native matching interface

manual trust layer

structured provider data

feedback flywheel

low-cost validation

potential expansion to other wellness hubs

The reason it might fail is:

customers just want cheap massage

providers do not need you

villa partners demand too much commission

safety/liability gets messy

AI matching does not outperform curation

Yuni does not like the field role

Marcus gets pulled into ops

So v5 should be built around testing those exact failure modes. That is what makes this real.
