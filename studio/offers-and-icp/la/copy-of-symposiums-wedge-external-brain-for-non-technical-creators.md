Symposium's Wedge

Making the external brain accessible to non-technical creators

The best first customers are people whose past work is already valuable, but trapped.

Serious writers, researchers, founders, filmmakers, coaches, and independent thinkers already have the raw material for an AI-native external brain: years of drafts, notes, journals, voice memos, PDFs, emails, transcripts, Google Drive folders, and half-finished projects. What they lack is the technical bridge that turns that archive into a living, queryable, useful thinking partner.

Symposium Studios' wedge: install the first high-trust personal corpus systems for non-technical creators, then productize the repeated patterns into a scalable company.

1. The wedge

The mass-market version may eventually become a full company: a friendly, governed, consumer-grade external brain that ingests a person's life archive and helps them think, write, remember, decide, and create. But the wedge is not mass market first.

The wedge is high-touch and narrow: install external-brain systems for people who already have a valuable corpus and already feel the pain of not being able to access it.

A non-technical creator with a giant Google Drive is not starting from zero. They are sitting on trapped intelligence.

The first customers are not people who need to be convinced that notes matter. They already know. They have spent years generating material. They have drafts, research, transcripts, screenshots, essays, voice memos, interviews, half-written books, and folders that feel spiritually or professionally important but practically inaccessible.

Symposium's job is to make that corpus usable.

2. The core problem

Creators already outsource memory to storage systems. Google Drive, Dropbox, Apple Notes, Notion, email, journals, and voice memos hold the raw archive. But storage is passive. It preserves files without helping the person perceive the pattern inside them.

The writer problem: I have years of drafts, but I cannot see the book I have been circling.

The researcher problem: I have PDFs, notes, citations, and interviews, but I cannot quickly ask the whole archive what it knows.

The founder problem: I have strategy docs, memos, calls, investor notes, and product fragments, but the company memory is scattered.

The coach/teacher problem: I have frameworks, session notes, talks, slides, and principles, but no living system that can help me teach from my own body of work.

The filmmaker/artist problem: I have scripts, scenes, influences, interviews, abandoned ideas, and production notes, but no governed creative memory layer.

The pain is not just productivity. It is loss of self-access. People have already produced more signal than they can consciously hold.

Suggested v1 package

A 1- to 2-week high-touch setup that turns a creator's existing archive into a governed, queryable, useful AI memory system.

Archive intake: map existing sources, file types, sensitive zones, and desired outcomes.

Corpus cleanup: identify what belongs in v1, what should stay out, and what needs structure.

Starter prompt library: custom prompts for writing, research, synthesis, memory, decision support, and project extraction.

Governance notes: what is included, what is excluded, privacy assumptions, data ownership, and safe-use boundaries.

Use guide: a short plain-English guide for how to work with the system without becoming dependent or sloppy.

Possible pricing ladder

Starter setup: $3,000 to $7,500 for a narrow corpus and functional demo.

Full high-touch setup: $10,000 to $25,000+ depending on archive size, sensitivity, custom interface, and ongoing advisory.

Ongoing retainer: $1,000 to $5,000/month for corpus updates, prompt evolution, new workflows, privacy support, and artifact generation.

Pricing should be tested in conversation. The important point is that this is not a low-ticket prompt pack. For the right client, this touches life work, creative output, company memory, and legacy.

9. Risks and guardrails

This category touches identity, memory, intellectual property, private writing, and sometimes therapeutic or relational material. Trust is the product. Governance cannot be an afterthought.

Required guardrails

Data ownership: client retains ownership of all source material and outputs unless otherwise contracted.

Consent: do not ingest third-party private material without permission or a lawful basis.

Scope boundaries: do not present as therapy, legal advice, medical advice, or estate planning unless qualified professionals are involved.

Sensitive zones: create excluded folders for material that should not be ingested.

Human authority: the system supports the creator; it does not decide for them or represent them without approval.

Security posture: start with conservative setups, clear access controls, and plain-English risk notes.

Legacy controls: for public figures or artists, define who can access the system after death or incapacity.

11. First 30-day action plan

The next step is not to build the whole company. The next step is to produce a credible demo, test the pitch with real corpus-rich people, and learn the repeated setup pattern.

1.	Build one safe demo corpus. Use public-domain material, synthetic notes, or an intentionally redacted Marcus corpus slice.

2.	Create a 3-minute demo script. Show the archive surfacing forgotten themes, project ideas, and voice-consistent outputs.

3.	Write a one-page offer. Personal Corpus OS Setup: who it is for, what it does, what it includes, what it does not include.

4.	Talk to 10 corpus-rich people. Writers, researchers, founders, coaches, filmmakers, and serious journalers.

5.	Document every objection. Especially privacy, setup complexity, cost, accuracy, and fear of being flattened by AI.

6.	Sell one audit. Do not overbuild before one person pays for the map.

7.	Convert the audit into a setup. Use the first paid client to learn what the delivery package actually requires.

12. Working thesis to carry forward

Symposium's wedge is not generic AI consulting. It is the human bridge between private archives and useful AI memory. The market begins with people whose past work already has value and whose future work depends on being able to access it.

The archive is the ore. The external brain is the forge. The first business is helping serious creators build the forge without needing to become engineers.

This is a wedge because it starts as a service, teaches the real workflows, builds trust with high-signal users, and reveals the product patterns. The long-term company may become mass-market. But the first move is intimate, high-touch, and specific.
