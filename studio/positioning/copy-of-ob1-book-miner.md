# Copy of OB1 Book Miner

> Imported from the source archive and converted to Markdown for searchability. Treat the source path as provenance, not as the current canonical location.
OB1 Book Miner

Turning Marcus’s corpus into finished books, public receipts, and product infrastructure

Working strategy document

Executive summary

OB1 Book Miner is the first practical wedge for Open Brain / OB1: a corpus-to-book system that reads Marcus’s archive, identifies the strongest latent book projects, maps the missing last mile, and helps assemble publishable manuscripts. The immediate problem is not an idea shortage. It is an assembly problem. The corpus already contains multiple books at roughly 60 to 70 percent formation. The missing layer is a machine that can see across the archive, cluster themes, retrieve the strongest passages, deduplicate drafts, identify gaps, and turn scattered field notes into finished artifacts.

The first goal is Marcus-first, not mass-market. Build the system around the actual Pattern Intimacy archive, Drive folders, book drafts, AI self-governance notes, daily journals, product logs, and public writing. Then extract the repeatable method for serious operators, creators, founders, consultants, and teams who also suffer from context sprawl and unfinished knowledge work.

1. The core insight

The screenshots of Drive are the proof: AI Pressure Valve Book, Pattern Intimacy, AI Self-Governance, Phronetics, Solo But Not Alone, Field Notes, Symposium Studios, Author OS, Software Projects, Hardware Prototyping, and related folders. These are not random notes. They are an unfinished publishing system. The work already exists in fragments, drafts, journal entries, frameworks, conversations, product decisions, and live operating logs.

The bottleneck is not more ideation. The bottleneck is seeing the whole body of work at once and extracting the books that are already latent inside it. A normal notes app cannot do this. A generic chatbot cannot do this reliably because it lacks stable access, source grounding, project memory, privacy boundaries, and a structured manuscript pipeline. OB1 Book Miner exists to solve that.

2. What OB1 Book Miner is

OB1 Book Miner is a local or private corpus-analysis layer that helps Marcus turn his archive into finished book projects. It is not just search. It is not just summarization. It is not a content generator. It is a publishing intelligence system that can map, compare, retrieve, organize, and assemble.

Archive reader: ingests selected Drive folders, exported chats, docs, journal entries, prompts, build logs, essays, and book drafts.

Book detector: finds candidate books already forming inside the corpus.

Passage miner: surfaces the strongest source passages and keeps them tied to origin files.

Draft assembler: turns fragments into chapter maps, section drafts, and manuscript candidates.

Gap analyst: names what is missing before a manuscript can ship.

Privacy and redaction layer: marks private, sensitive, relational, medical, or legally risky content before public use.

Receipt engine: exports concrete artifacts: book maps, manuscript drafts, KDP-ready drafts, case studies, and product demos.

3. Why this matters now

The current strategic pressure is simple: Marcus needs public receipts that match private capability. The archive is rich, but the market cannot see it yet. Substack posts are useful, but they are not enough. A finished book on Amazon, even a short serious one, is a durable artifact. It gives people something to understand, reference, buy, cite, and hand around. It also forces last-mile thinking. A book cannot remain pure potential.

OB1 Book Miner also creates a double receipt. First, it can produce books. Second, the process of producing books from the archive becomes a case study for OB1 itself: a system that reads a complex human corpus and helps convert it into finished work.

6. MVP: Book Miner V1

The MVP should be brutally practical. It should answer one question: which book can Marcus finish first, and what exact source material should be used to finish it?

1.	Select 3 to 5 high-priority folders from Drive for the first ingestion pass.

2.	Create a manifest of files with title, folder, modified date, rough word count, and project tag.

3.	Chunk and index the documents with source IDs and paragraph-level references where possible.

4.	Cluster chunks by recurring themes, concepts, and book candidates.

5.	Generate a candidate book list with evidence: strongest source docs, repeated concepts, best passages, missing sections.

6.	Choose one first book.

7.	Generate a chapter map and source ledger.

8.	Assemble a V1 manuscript draft from sourced material, not generic generation.

9.	Mark private or sensitive sections for redaction or rewrite.

10.	Export DOCX and Markdown drafts for human editing.

7. System architecture

The first version can be simple. It does not need a beautiful app. It needs a reliable ingestion pipeline, a source ledger, a retrieval layer, and a manuscript assembler. A rough local tool or developer-facing interface is enough if it produces real artifacts.

Layer

Function

Notes

Corpus import

Pull selected folders and files into a local working directory.

Start with exported Google Docs or Drive downloads if direct API is too slow.

Metadata manifest

Track file name, folder, date, type, word count, candidate project, and privacy level.

This becomes the source ledger.

Chunking

Split documents into useful passages with source IDs.

Prefer semantic paragraphs or sections over arbitrary tiny chunks.

Indexing

Create searchable representations for retrieval and clustering.

Can start with local embeddings or simple hybrid search.

Theme clustering

Group passages by concept, book candidate, and repeated frame.

Important for finding latent books.

Book map engine

Generate candidate outlines from source evidence.

Should cite source IDs internally.

Gap engine

Identify missing chapters, weak sections, and unresolved claims.

This turns archive into task list.

Manuscript compiler

Assemble selected passages and generated bridge prose into DOCX/Markdown.

Must preserve voice and source provenance.

Privacy review

Flag sensitive names, legal details, medical details, family/relationship material.

Required before public export.

8. Outputs OB1 Book Miner should generate

Corpus inventory: a map of what exists by folder, project, date, and approximate size.

Book candidate report: ranked list of possible books with evidence and readiness scores.

Book map: working title, thesis, audience, promise, chapter outline, and source documents.

Passage bank: best excerpts organized by chapter or theme, with source references.

Gap list: missing sections, weak arguments, needed stories, needed definitions, needed redactions.

Manuscript V1: a readable first draft assembled from the archive.

KDP readiness checklist: title, subtitle, description, categories, front matter, back matter, cover direction, redaction status.

Case study: how OB1 converted archive into manuscript, useful for Symposium and future customers.

10. The first book hypothesis

The likely first target is The AI Pressure Valve or a tightly scoped AI Self-Governance book. This is not necessarily the deepest book. It may be the best first public receipt because it is timely, short, concrete, and directly connected to the central thesis: AI can function as a self-governance tool, pressure valve, pattern mirror, and external working memory.

A possible working title: The AI Pressure Valve: Self-Governance in the Age of Intelligent Systems. The book can be short, serious, and framework-driven. It can explain the practice without over-exposing private life. Pattern Intimacy can remain the deeper masterwork, built later after the system has matured.

12. Productization path after Marcus-first version

After OB1 Book Miner works for Marcus, the product can be generalized carefully. The first wedge is not everyone. The first wedge is serious operators who already feel the pain.

Founders with scattered notes, calls, docs, investor updates, product decisions, and AI threads.

Creators and writers with years of unpublished drafts, journals, essays, and frameworks.

Consultants and fractional executives with client context spread across docs, email, calls, and chatbots.

AI-native operators who already use ChatGPT, Claude, Notion, Drive, GitHub, and Slack but lose continuity across tools.

Recovery, coaching, or self-governance users only after privacy and ethics are much stronger.

The first commercial language should stay grounded: I help founder-led teams and serious operators stop losing operational context across AI tools, docs, and projects. We build a lightweight AI operating layer around the actual workflow.
