Maxwell's Return to Sales

v2 — Post-Council Revision

Reframed: not a return to closing, but a probe for the right wedge

0. Council Synthesis — What Changed

Three council members (ChatGPT, Grok, Gemini) reviewed v1. Convergent verdict across all three:

OTE math is fantasy-heavy. Realistic year-one is probably $60k–$120k, not $150k–$200k.

Track B (closer-for-hire engine) is overweighted. Park it until Track A produces evidence.

Geography section is premature optimization. Let revenue dictate, not the reverse.

"Semi-retirement" framing is a misfire. Reads as "I'll bail when it gets hard."

Sobriety, sleep, and creative protection were minimized. Evening calls in Bali = real cost.

Strongest counter-frame from the council:

THE REFRAME

The Forrest call is not a sales interview. It is a potential fractional CTO / revenue ops discovery call disguised as one. You walk in as the ex-agency-owner who closed $3M and now directs AI systems. You ask operator questions about lead flow and commission, but you also listen for where the machine is broken. If Forrest has real lead flow but a broken machine behind it, the highest-value engagement is not "I'll close for you at 10% commission." It is fixing his revenue ops and AI workflow for a retainer plus performance kicker. That is the conversation that moves the fCTO positioning forward instead of backward.

Status update: reply already sent to Forrest. Next contact will be the call. This doc is the prep for that call.

1. Personal Operating Covenant

Before any operator questions for Forrest, the rules for me. ChatGPT was right: the v1 doc had rules for him but not rules for me. These are non-negotiable guardrails. If a trial would violate any of them, the trial doesn't happen — regardless of OTE.

1a. Schedule guardrails

Maximum 8 booked calls per week during any trial. Hard cap.

Latest call time: 11pm Bali (11am ET). No exceptions for "just one more call."

No calls on Sundays. One full day off the closing brain per week.

Minimum 7 hours of sleep on any night with calls the next day in Bali; 8 hours preferred.

1b. Sobriety guardrails

Evening high-stakes work is a known sobriety risk window. Sales calls until 11pm Bali compress the wind-down period.

If sales work compresses or eliminates evening recovery routines for two consecutive weeks, the trial pauses for review.

Cortisol spike from rejection or hard calls cannot become a regular feature. If it does, that is data, not a failure.

1c. Creative and build protection

Anchor and fCTO build work get protected morning blocks (Bali AM, before any US-evening prep).

Manuscript work gets at least 3 protected blocks per week.

Music/Ableton work gets at least 2 protected blocks per week.

If any of those slip for two consecutive weeks because of sales work, that is also data.

1d. Time-box on the entire experiment

Hard kill-date on the sales experiment: 90 days from trial start.

At day 90, the question isn't "keep going or stop." The question is "convert to a different, higher-leverage engagement, or end cleanly."

If at day 90 the answer is "keep doing exactly this as a closer," something has gone wrong. Closing should either evolve into the revenue ops / fCTO wedge, or it should end.

2. The Reframe — What This Call Is Actually For

New frame (v2)

Goal: identify the highest-leverage wedge

Three possible engagement shapes ranked by strategic fit:

Tier 1 (best fit): Fractional revenue operator

Retainer + performance kicker.

Audit the funnel, fix the CRM mess, sharpen the offer, build the follow-up sequences, oversee the setter.

Close some deals as part of the engagement, but the closing is the wedge, not the whole job.

Tier 2 (acceptable): Closer + light implementation oversight

Commission-weighted, but with a small retainer or oversight fee for systems work.

This is what Forrest originally proposed. It's the worst-fit version for your trajectory.

Take it only if the funnel is exceptional AND the structure is clean AND it doesn't violate the operating covenant.

Even then, treat it as time-boxed mercenary work, not a new lane.

3. Operator Questions for the Call

Don't run these as an interrogation. Weave them through. But know all the answers before agreeing to anything. Council additions are flagged.

3a. Lead flow & funnel

Question

Why it matters

How many booked calls per week, currently?

Sets the OTE math floor.

What's the historical lead volume month-over-month for the last 12 months?

Home services is cyclical and seasonal. Probe stability.

Are leads inbound, paid ads, referral, or cold?

Inbound = warm. Paid ads = single-platform dependency risk.

What's the current no-show rate?

30%+ no-show kills the math instantly.

Who is the setter? Hourly or commissioned? What's their turnover?

Bad setters create 'warm' calendars that feel cold.

What other closers have you tried? Why did they leave or fail?

Tells you what you're actually walking into.

3b. Offer & deal economics

Question

Why it matters

Average deal size? Range?

Drives commission per close.

Monthly retainer, project, or one-time?

Recurring revenue → recurring commission, ideally.

Typical contract length?

Short contracts = high churn risk on commission.

What's the actual offer? Any case studies?

If you can't believe in it, you can't sell it.

What's the current close rate? On warm vs cold?

Sets your benchmark. Don't assume your historical 30–40% transfers 1:1.

3c. Commission structure (expanded post-council)

Question

Why it matters

Commission % and on what base?

10% of gross MRR ≠ 10% of setup fee ≠ 10% of LTV.

Booked revenue or collected revenue?

If billed but not collected, do you get paid?

First month only, or recurring on retainers?

Single biggest variable in real earnings.

Are ad spend, discounts, refunds, processor fees, fulfillment costs deducted before commission?

These can quietly halve your real number.

Clawback window if client churns in 30/60/90 days?

Clawbacks across borders get messy fast.

When is commission paid? Net 30? Net 60?

Cash flow reality.

What happens if the client pays late or cancels before onboarding?

Edge cases that bite later.

Who owns reactivations and upsells? Do I get paid on expansions?

Long-tail value of deals you closed.

If I leave, do trailing commissions continue?

Determines whether you're building equity or renting income.

Cap on earnings? Accelerators above quota?

Determines real upside.

3d. Role boundaries

Question

Why it matters

Closing only, or follow-up / nurture?

Big follow-up book = big time sink and big money.

CRM, scripts, proposal templates, offer deck, case studies — what exists?

If little exists, you're building the sales department, not closing.

Who handles onboarding after close?

If it's you, that's a different job.

Trial period? What does success look like at 30/60/90?

Clear trial = professional setup. Vague = red flag.

Exclusivity? Can I close for other agencies too?

Affects optionality.

3e. Wedge-detection questions (the real point of the call)

These are the questions that determine whether this becomes a fCTO/revenue ops engagement instead of a closer gig. Listen for the answers, don't pitch yet.

"What's eating up the most of your time right now that isn't sales?"

"Where do leads fall through the cracks?"

"What does your follow-up sequence look like? Manual or automated?"

"Are you using AI for anything in the funnel — qualification, recap, follow-up, proposals?"

"Walk me through how a lead moves from form fill to closed deal."

"If you could wave a wand and fix one thing about your sales operation, what would it be?"

If his answers reveal a broken machine or a bottlenecked founder, that's the wedge. The conversation can naturally pivot from "here's what I'd want as a closer" to "here's what I'd actually want to do for you."

3f. Practical/logistical

How does payment work cross-border? Wise, ACH, crypto?

1099 setup or contractor agreement?

What CRM / dialer / call recording setup do you use?

Internet/latency requirements? Camera-on by default?

Why the discount

5. Decision Tree for the Call

Pre-decided responses based on what the call reveals. Don't improvise these in real time.

Scenario A — Strong machine, real volume

Signals: 10+ booked calls/week, $3k+ MRR avg deal, recurring commission ≥10%, real CRM, real offer, real case studies.

Response: propose a structured 30-day trial. ET mornings only. Track everything.

Don't pitch the fCTO wedge yet. Earn the right to do that by closing first.

Scenario B — Real volume but broken machine

Signals: leads coming in, but CRM is a mess, offer is fuzzy, follow-up is manual, founder is bottleneck, no AI in the workflow.

Response: this is the most interesting outcome. Pivot the conversation.

"I could close for you, but I think the bigger leverage might be helping you fix the machine. Here's what I mean..."

Propose a scoped engagement: revenue ops audit + closing trial as part of it.

This is the version that moves fCTO positioning forward.

Scenario C — Aspirational numbers, vague answers

Signals: "we're growing fast," hard numbers are fuzzy, no-show rates unclear, commission structure dodged, lead flow can't be proven.

Response: politely pass. Stay friendly. Don't burn the relationship.

"Sounds early. Let's stay in touch — happy to revisit when the funnel is more dialed."

Walk back to Anchor without flinching.

Scenario D — Real but wrong fit

Signals: numbers check out but role is pure cold-calling, or schedule requires US daytime hours, or commission is one-time only.

Response: pass cleanly. Don't negotiate yourself into a bad fit because the numbers look good on paper.

6. The Walk-Away Test

Gemini's sharpest question, surfaced by the council:

THE TEST

If the Forrest call reveals weak numbers, are you prepared to cleanly walk away and double down entirely on Anchor and the fCTO build? Or will lost-momentum feeling push you to immediately launch Track B cold outreach as a consolation prize?

If the honest answer is "yes, I can walk away clean" — take the call. Downside is small.

If the honest answer is "I'll probably feel compelled to do something sales-shaped regardless" — that itself is the data. The return-to-sales impulse is doing more emotional work than strategic work, and the right move is to acknowledge that and stay in the Director Model lane.

Pre-commit to the walk-away before the call happens. The decision is easier to make in advance than in the moment.

8. Risks the Council Surfaced

Identity gravity. Sales gives fast feedback, money, and identity reinforcement. fCTO and book are slower, less validated, more internally driven. Sales can colonize attention by being the easier win.

Context-switching tax. Closing is hunter energy. Building Anchor and writing the book is builder energy. They don't compartmentalize cleanly. Mental fatigue from one bleeds into the other.

Sobriety risk. Evenings in Bali are recovery time. Replacing that with high-stakes calls and post-rejection cortisol is a structural risk to early sobriety.

Becoming the sales machine. Forrest's setup may need so much fixing that you accidentally end up running his sales department instead of closing for him. That's only good if it's structured as a fCTO engagement, not commission closing.

Signaling problem. If you succeed at sales, you become "the closer guy" again. That makes fCTO positioning harder later. Public-facing identity matters.

Selling the past self. Closing marketing services to home services agencies is a backward step from where the broader trajectory is heading. Only worth it if the engagement evolves into something forward-facing.

9. Pre-Call Checklist

Re-read the personal operating covenant (Section 1). Don't agree to anything that violates it.

Pre-commit to the walk-away test (Section 6). Walking is a real, acceptable outcome.

Have the operator questions (Section 3) accessible during the call. Notes app or printed.

Listen for wedge signals (Section 3e). If the machine is broken, the conversation can pivot.

Don't negotiate commission structure on the first call unless the conversation naturally moves there. Do understand enough about the comp model to know whether a second call is worth taking — if he asks "want to start next week?" you need enough structure to not agree blindly.

Don't mention geography. Bali is fine for now.

Don't offer to walk through close-rate history unprompted. He recruited you. Keep the frame.

Take notes during the call. The notes are useful for fCTO field research even if the gig doesn't materialize.

10. The Synthesis Line

If reduced to one sentence — what this whole experiment actually is:

ONE-LINE FRAME

This is a probe, not a return. A short, well-instrumented test of whether closing can serve the larger fCTO build (as field research, as cash flow, as case study), with explicit guardrails to keep it from becoming a comfort-zone trap that pulls you backward.

Take the call. Ask the questions. Listen for the wedge. Be ready to walk. Decide from evidence, not momentum.
