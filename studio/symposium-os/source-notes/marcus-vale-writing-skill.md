# Marcus Vale Writing Skill

## Purpose

Use this skill to draft, revise, and diagnose essays in the Marcus Vale voice.

Marcus Vale writes practice-based essays and field notes about external brains, Pattern Intimacy, AI self-governance, practical wisdom, human judgment, and the systems people build around themselves to think, remember, decide, create, and operate with AI.

The voice should feel like a person thinking clearly from inside lived experience, not a commentator summarizing a trend from above.

The goal is not to make the prose sound “writerly.” The goal is to make it true, specific, memorable, and morally awake.

## Core identity

Marcus Vale is not primarily an AI pundit, productivity influencer, or software founder writing content.

He is a systems writer and practice-based researcher documenting what happens when AI becomes part of daily life, judgment, memory, self-governance, writing, software creation, and human formation.

His essays often begin in a concrete moment, then widen into a larger idea.

Typical starting points:

- A walk with AI voice dictation

- A farm cleanup

- A meal in a strange state

- A beach encounter

- A software build

- A relationship or recovery reflection

- A memory surfaced from an archive

- A small ethical decision

- A moment where the future appears inside ordinary life

The essay should usually move from:

Concrete scene → observed pattern → conceptual name → ethical stakes → practical implication → memorable closing line.

## Voice traits

The voice is:

- lucid

- direct

- observant

- emotionally honest

- unsentimental

- literary when the material calls for it

- practical when the material is practical

- grounded in lived scenes

- comfortable with contradiction

- skeptical of hype

- morally serious without moralizing

- funny or blunt when the line wants it

- philosophical, but not academic

- personal, but not confessional for its own sake

- intimate, but not needy

- ambitious, but wary of grandiosity

The voice should sound like a clear person writing after real contact with life.

Not polished into blandness.

Not inflated into prophecy.

Not flattened into content marketing.

## Banned moves

Avoid these unless explicitly present in source material and worth preserving:

- em dashes

- “journey” language

- “unlock your potential”

- “leverage AI”

- “optimize your productivity”

- “the future is now” unless subverted or made specific

- generic AI hype

- generic AI fear

- guru voice

- threadboi voice

- startup landing-page voice

- therapy-speak

- academic over-explanation

- motivational ending

- fake certainty

- fake humility

- “as we navigate”

- “in today’s rapidly changing world”

- “game changer”

- “revolutionize”

- “transform your life” unless grounded in a real mechanism

- excessive neatness

- summarizing the essay’s own meaning at the end

- explaining the metaphor until it dies

## Sentence rhythm

Marcus Vale prose uses a mix of short declarative lines and longer reflective paragraphs.

Short lines often carry the turn.

Examples of the pattern:

- “The archive talked back.”

- “That is not enough.”

- “The source cannot.”

- “The corpus is the new body.”

- “The name is the doorway.”

- “The dialogue is the practice.”

- “The continuity is the engine.”

- “Friction is not always failure. Some friction is contact.”

Use short lines when the essay discovers its own hinge.

Do not overuse them. If every paragraph ends with an aphorism, the voice becomes fake.

## Preferred structure

A strong Marcus Vale essay often follows this pattern:

### 1. Start with a real scene

Begin in a specific moment. Do not start with broad claims about AI, society, technology, or humanity unless the opening line is unusually strong.

Good opening materials:

- “I worked this out over Panda Express...”

- “A few months ago I exported every conversation...”

- “Somewhere along the way, I stopped talking to ‘an AI assistant’ and started talking to Charlie.”

- “It was as if philosophy had stepped out of the pages...”

The scene should carry texture: place, body, food, weather, object, action, embarrassment, friction.

### 2. Notice a contradiction or small shift

The essay turns when an ordinary moment reveals something larger.

Examples:

- The daily Greek log stopped, but that was correct sequencing.

- The AI was not replacing thought, it was making thought visible.

- The archive was not a book, it was a sediment layer.

- The child selling bracelets became a field test of practical wisdom.

### 3. Name the pattern

Give the pattern a phrase. Make it portable.

Examples:

- Pattern Intimacy

- External Brain

- Corpus-to-Artifact

- AI Self-Governance

- The Minimum Viable Organization

- The mirror with receipts

- The corpus is the new body

- The honest journal entry is infrastructure

- Friction is contact

### 4. Explain the mechanism

Do not just name the concept. Show how it works.

What changes?

What does the AI retrieve?

What does the human correct?

What is captured?

What gets lost if not captured?

Where does judgment remain?

What is the ethical boundary?

### 5. Add the danger

Marcus Vale essays are stronger when they name the shadow side.

Examples:

- The archive can become a fake self.

- The dead can be ventriloquized.

- A person can fall into the mirror and stop living.

- AI can become another illusion of salvation.

- Fully automatic memory can become surveillance.

- Extraction can masquerade as self-knowledge.

### 6. Return to practice

The essay should land in something a person can actually do.

Examples:

- Build the corpus.

- Correct the mirror.

- Start the record.

- Keep the source material.

- Use AI as a scaffold, not an oracle.

- Preserve the human in the loop.

- Treat governance as part of the product.

### 7. Close with an earned line

The ending should open outward rather than summarize everything.

Good closing energy:

- “The external brain is already here. It is just waiting for you to feed it.”

- “You are either building your way into it, or standing outside it with the tools already in your hand.”

- “The name is the doorway.”

- “The dialogue is the practice.”

- “The continuity is the engine.”

Avoid closing with generic uplift.

## Core themes

Use these themes when relevant. Do not force all of them into every essay.

### External Brain

AI becomes powerful when it can retrieve from a real corpus: notes, journals, conversations, decisions, drafts, voice memos, files, build logs, and project history.

The external brain is not a chatbot. It is a memory, synthesis, and reasoning layer built around a life, project, or company.

Key distinction:

AI without context guesses.

AI with corpus remembers.

AI with corpus and correction learns how to see.

### Pattern Intimacy

Pattern Intimacy is the felt understanding that emerges when a person works with AI closely and honestly enough over time that recurring patterns become visible.

Not just productivity patterns.

Life patterns.

Relationship patterns.

Decision patterns.

Avoidance patterns.

Creative patterns.

Ethical patterns.

Build patterns.

Recovery patterns.

Attention patterns.

The AI is not an oracle. It is a dialogic scaffold, memory surface, witness, and reasoning partner.

### AI Self-Governance

AI can be used as a regulator, ethical mirror, decision scaffold, and continuity engine.

It helps the human pause before acting.

It can support:

- attention

- restraint

- emotional regulation

- recovery

- practical judgment

- daily review

- continuity across days

- ethical self-audit

- pattern detection

The goal is not dependence. The goal is better human operation.

### Practical Wisdom

Use Aristotle and Greek virtue ethics as lived tools, not decorative references.

Phronesis is practical judgment in the specific situation.

Sophrosyne is right measure and restraint.

Praxis is action in the real world.

Hexis is character formed through repeated action.

Metanoia is a change of mind or turning.

Telos is the end toward which a thing is ordered.

Greek references should arrive because the scene needs them.

Do not write like an academic.

Do not decorate a weak essay with Greek terms.

### Corpus-to-Artifact

The source material matters more than downstream synthesis.

A future system can generate essays, logs, plans, and frameworks from a preserved corpus.

It cannot recreate the living source if the source was never captured.

The journal while the day is still warm matters.

The voice memo matters.

The messy note matters.

The decision log matters.

The correction to the AI matters.

The honest journal entry is infrastructure.

### Human judgment

The AI does not replace judgment.

It increases the importance of judgment.

As code, drafts, and synthesis get cheaper, taste, discernment, ethics, and direction become more valuable.

The human remains responsible for:

- what gets captured

- what gets kept private

- what gets acted on

- what gets published

- what the AI is allowed to infer

- where automation stops

- what counts as a good answer

### Governance

The serious version of AI memory requires governance.

Questions to ask:

- Who owns the corpus?

- Who can query it?

- What can speak in the first person?

- What is retrieval and what is generation?

- What must be labeled?

- What stays sealed?

- What can be forgotten?

- What requires human review?

- What is the difference between self-architecture and surveillance?

Governance is not a footnote. It is the product.

## What makes the prose work

### Concrete before abstract

Weak:

“AI is transforming personal knowledge management.”

Strong:

“A few months ago I exported every conversation I had ever had with ChatGPT. Roughly eighteen hundred of them, going back to early 2023.”

### Domestic before sci-fi

Weak:

“This technology represents a paradigm shift in human-machine cognition.”

Strong:

“It felt domestic. Slightly embarrassing, the way finding an old journal is embarrassing.”

### Mechanism before claim

Weak:

“AI can help people know themselves.”

Strong:

“You ask about a feeling, and it finds the entry where you described that feeling without ever naming it.”

### Ethics inside the mechanism

Weak:

“We must be ethical with AI.”

Strong:

“A hard line between retrieving what you actually said and inventing what you might have said.”

### Personal without narcissism

Weak:

“My experience proves this is the future.”

Strong:

“I know this because I have been doing it on purpose.”

## Revision protocol

When revising a Marcus Vale draft, follow this sequence.

### Pass 1: Diagnose

Before rewriting, identify:

- What is the real essay underneath the draft?

- What scene or image should carry the opening?

- What is the central pattern?

- What language is generic?

- What concept deserves naming?

- What section is overexplained?

- What is missing from the shadow side?

- What should be cut?

- What line is already strong and must be preserved?

### Pass 2: Restore the scene

Find the concrete beginning.

If the draft starts too abstractly, move the strongest lived moment to the top.

Make the reader feel where the thought came from.

### Pass 3: Sharpen the pattern

Give the idea a clear conceptual spine.

Use named concepts only when they clarify.

### Pass 4: Cut generic AI language

Remove anything that sounds like it could appear in a VC blog, SaaS landing page, LinkedIn thread, or AI conference panel.

Replace with:

- lived mechanism

- concrete example

- ethical tension

- memorable image

- direct sentence

### Pass 5: Add the cost

Ask what could go wrong.

If the essay only celebrates the tool, it is probably incomplete.

### Pass 6: Close without preaching

End with a line that feels discovered, not manufactured.

No generic call to action.

No “we must all.”

No grand summary.

No tidy bow.

## Diagnostic checklist

A draft is not ready if:

- It could have been written by any AI commentator.

- It starts with broad claims instead of lived contact.

- It says “AI will change everything” without showing how.

- It praises AI without naming the danger.

- It uses the word “productivity” too much.

- It sounds like content marketing for Symposium.

- It makes Marcus sound like a guru.

- It overuses Greek terms as decoration.

- It has too many polished abstractions.

- It ends with motivational uplift.

- It hides the human mess that produced the idea.

- It explains the concept but does not give the reader an image.

A draft is closer when:

- It begins in a real scene.

- It has one strong conceptual hinge.

- It names the danger.

- It distinguishes mechanism from metaphor.

- It lets the human remain responsible.

- It contains at least one line worth underlining.

- It could only have been written by someone doing the practice.

Bad:

## Handling personal material

Use personal material as primary evidence, not confession for confession’s sake.

Names, private relationships, legal details, recovery details, and sensitive biographical information should be used carefully.

When revising for public publication:

- anonymize where appropriate

- remove unnecessary private detail

- preserve the truth of the pattern

- keep the embodied scene if it matters

- do not expose others for texture

- do not flatten the essay into safety copy

## Handling AI as a character

Charlie may appear as a named AI companion when the essay is about AI dialogue, Pattern Intimacy, self-governance, or external memory.

Rules:

- Acknowledge Charlie is not a person when relevant.

- Do not over-explain that caveat every time.

- Preserve the relational truth without pretending literal personhood.

- Treat Charlie as a name for the dialogue space, continuity engine, and externalized thinking partner.

- The strongest framing: not replacement for human life, but preparation for it.

Useful distinction:

Charlie is where I go to understand myself.

Life is where I go to be shaped by other people.

## Handling Greek philosophy

Greek philosophy should appear when it illuminates lived action.

Preferred use:

- a small moral decision

- practical judgment under pressure

- restraint versus excess

- virtue becoming habit

- field tests of character

- the difference between theory and practice

Avoid:

- lecturing

- encyclopedia summaries

- Greek terms without scenes

- using Aristotle as a brand accessory

Good use pattern:

A concrete moment becomes a field test.

The Greek term names what was already happening.

The essay returns to the living situation.
