Version 0.1 Skeleton - May 2026

1. Executive Thesis

Symposium Studios is an AI-native product studio built around two complementary prongs: client work and first-party IP. The studio builds serious tools, apps, prototypes, internal systems, and agentic workflows for clients while also developing proprietary products, frameworks, and interactive experiences that it brings to market itself.

Core Thesis

Client work creates cashflow, market intelligence, proof, and distribution. First-party IP creates equity, leverage, and long-term studio value. The two prongs should reinforce each other without collapsing into generic agency chaos.

• The timing matters: the market is rapidly waking up to agentic coding and AI-native product development.

• The founder has unusual background fit: prior agency ownership, engineering history, product taste, AI workflow fluency, and current proof artifacts.

• The studio should move quickly, but not recklessly. Speed must be governed by proof, contracts, focus, and operating discipline.

• The studio should not sell “we build anything for anyone.” It should sell a sharp wedge first, then expand from a position of receipts.

2. Working Definition

3. The Two-Prong Business Model

The studio has two prongs by design. They should be treated as complementary, not competing.

3.1 Prong One: Client Studio

• Builds paid tools, apps, prototypes, internal systems, dashboards, workflows, and AI-assisted operations systems for external clients.

• Generates near-term cashflow and client proof.

• Creates market contact: real pain, real budgets, real constraints, real language.

• Trains apprentices on bounded production tasks without exposing the full core IP too early.

• Produces case studies that make the studio credible in New York, Bali, online, and with founder networks.

3.2 Prong Two: First-Party IP Lab

• Builds studio-owned products, games, assessments, internal platforms, education assets, and software frameworks.

• Turns the founder’s archive, frameworks, recovery work, build OS, and AI-native experiments into owned assets.

• Creates long-term equity instead of only trading time for client revenue.

• Uses client work as research and distribution, but does not let client work consume all product capacity.

• Protects the distinctive agentic methodology as proprietary studio IP.

4. Why Now

The operating assumption is that AI-native building is crossing from novelty into practical business infrastructure. Speed matters because the market is still confused, but the window will not stay under-defined forever.

• Agentic coding is becoming legible: Codex, Claude Code, Gemini, Replit, Cursor, and other systems are making small-team software output dramatically faster.

• Most companies still do not know how to specify, supervise, test, and deploy AI-built systems.

• The bottleneck is shifting from raw coding labor to judgment, taste, product direction, QA, deployment discipline, and trust boundaries.

• The founder’s advantage is not only using tools. It is directing multiple tools, preserving context, producing artifacts, and converting chaotic ideas into shipped software.

• First-mover advantage is real only if it becomes proof, revenue, case studies, and repeatable operating method.

6. What Used To Happen: The Old Agency Pattern

This section should name the old pattern clearly so Symposium Studios does not unconsciously recreate it with better tools.

• Broad service positioning: saying yes to too many client types, too many project types, and too many custom requests.

• Delivery drag: project management, unclear specs, handoff friction, client ambiguity, vendor dependency, and slow cycles eating founder energy.

• Founder isolation: the founder having to hold too much judgment, context, sales pressure, and technical responsibility alone.

• Relationship-based hiring: bringing people in based on history, loyalty, potential, or emotional trust before there is clear execution proof.

• Revenue without compounding: services cashflow that keeps the machine alive but does not always create durable IP or leverage.

6.1 What Changes Now

• The studio uses AI agents as force multipliers, not as magic replacements for judgment.

• The founder operates as strategic director / fCTO / product showrunner rather than heads-down solo implementer for every line of code.

• Work is structured into scoped sprints, proof artifacts, case studies, and reusable internal methods.

• People are brought in through gated apprenticeship, not assumed trust.

• First-party IP is protected and developed alongside client cashflow.

8. Client Wedge and ICP

The initial wedge should be narrow enough to become known for something, but broad enough to capture real demand.

8.1 Suggested Initial Wedge

Wedge Statement

AI-native internal tools and MVP sprints for founders, operators, and small teams who need working software fast but do not have the right technical/product infrastructure.

8.2 Best Early ICPs

• Founder-led businesses with messy operations and enough budget to pay for relief.

• Agencies, consultants, and operators drowning in workflow fragmentation.

• Wellness, education, recovery, coaching, retreat, and community businesses where tools can improve operations and trust.

• Remote founders and Bali/NYC network nodes who understand software value but need a fast technical partner.

• Existing Eagle Rocket or warm-network clients where trust already exists and a paid AI audit can be sold with low friction.

8.3 No-Go Clients

• Clients who want vague “AI magic” with no operational owner.

• Clients with no budget, no urgency, and no decision-maker access.

• Clients asking for autonomous outbound, regulated workflows, medical/legal/financial advice, or payment-moving systems without serious review.

• Clients likely to pull the studio into unlimited custom support or unclear scope.

• Clients who demand ownership of the studio’s underlying method, templates, or internal build OS.

11.2 Build Cycle

• Define: problem, user, acceptance criteria, no-go scope, data/safety boundaries.

• Spec: create autonomous build directive with context, phases, tests, manual review gates, and fallback content.

• Execute: agent builds in bounded scope with branch/file discipline.

• Verify: automated tests where available, build/typecheck/lint, manual smoke, production smoke when relevant.

• Review: Council/model review for safety, product, UX, architecture, and hidden risk.

• Package: commit, changelog, screenshots, short case-study notes, next-step list.

12. Apprenticeship Model

The apprenticeship model is a major potential advantage, but only if it is gated. The studio needs sharp prodigies, not emotionally selected helpers or unbounded collaborators.

12.1 Apprenticeship Thesis

Apprenticeship Thesis

Marcus can teach the right person to operate the AI-native build stack, but the method is valuable IP. Access should be earned through proof, trust, confidentiality, and execution quality.

12.2 Entry Gates

• NDA and IP assignment before exposure to private templates, client work, or the deeper build OS.

• Paid trial task with narrow scope and clear acceptance criteria.

• Evidence of responsiveness, judgment, self-correction, documentation, and emotional steadiness.

• Ability to follow the autonomous OS without requiring constant founder regulation.

• Gradual access: public tasks first, then internal tools, then client surfaces, then core method.

14. IP, Confidentiality, and Contracts

The method is part of the product. The studio must protect client data, first-party IP, build templates, training material, and internal operating procedures.

• Every collaborator who touches private studio material signs NDA and IP assignment.

• Client contracts distinguish between client-owned deliverables and studio-owned underlying methods, templates, tools, prompts, systems, and pre-existing IP.

• No client receives the full internal agentic build OS by default.

• No sensitive client data is sent into AI systems without explicit consent and clear data-handling rules.

• Outbound communication, payments, medical/legal/financial advice, and high-risk automation require human approval gates.

• Case studies require either permission, anonymization, or careful use of internal/non-client examples.

16. Revenue Logic

The studio should avoid both undercharging and overcomplicating the model. Early pricing should be simple enough to quote and high enough to preserve founder energy.

• Early cashflow can come from audits and prototype sprints.

• Higher-leverage revenue comes from implementations, retainers, licensing, training, and first-party products.

• Client work should be selected partly by whether it generates reusable learning, case studies, or product insight.

• First-party IP should eventually reduce dependence on client services.

• Revenue should fund proof, app store milestones, better tooling, and selective talent, not premature overhead.
