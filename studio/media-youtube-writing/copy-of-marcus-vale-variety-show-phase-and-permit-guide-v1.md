# Copy of Marcus Vale Variety Show Phase and Permit Guide v1

> Imported from the source archive and converted to Markdown for searchability. Treat the source path as provenance, not as the current canonical location.
THE MARCUS VALE VARIETY SHOW

Venice Public Stage Phase Guide

Build the ritual before the media machine. Test like a street artist. Scale like a professional.

Version 1 - June 1, 2026

Purpose
This guide turns the Marcus Vale Variety Show from a vision into a staged operating plan: start as public play, gather real signal, add documentation only when it helps the ritual, and formalize permits once the show becomes a repeatable public production.

This is not legal advice. It is an operating guide based on the current public rules and official source pages reviewed for Venice Beach, LA Recreation and Parks, and FilmLA. Confirm the latest rules with Venice Beach Recreation Center and FilmLA before operating.

Executive Summary

The Marcus Vale Variety Show should not begin as a full filmed production. It should begin as a low-pressure public co-creation ritual, then grow toward media capture and permits only as the format proves itself.

The soul of MVS is co-creation, not content extraction.

The first proof is not views. The first proof is aliveness: do strangers stop, feel safe, and make something with Marcus?

Cameras should document the magic after the magic exists. They should not become the reason the interaction happens.

Venice is not permissionless. It is more permission-accessible than New York. The Boardwalk has public-expression rules, designated spaces, and filming rules.

The best progression is Phase -1, Phase 0, Phase 1, Phase 2, Phase 3: prepare, test the ritual, add ambient documentation, add participant-aware filming, then formalize the production.

Note: The confidence-preserving frame is: the city is not saying no. It is saying learn the rules of the stage. Start light enough that the first tests are about aliveness, not bureaucracy.

Foundation: The Show Ethos

The Marcus Vale Variety Show is not a fixed bit. It is a format engine. The repeatable format is that Marcus creates a temporary public stage, a stranger enters, and something gets made.

Not gawking. Co-creation.

Not humiliation. Dignity and play.

Not poverty content. Ordinary hospitality stays ordinary and off-camera unless the person actively wants to participate.

Not one niche. Range made legible.

Not a gear fantasy. A ritual that can later become media.

Not lawless. Playful, respectful, and permit-aware.

The Public Promise

A person walks up. Marcus learns their name, reads the energy, and creates a small live format around them: a song, a drum lesson, an AI demo, a joke, a street philosophy prompt, a painting, a tiny band, a public experiment, or a simple conversation.

The Internal Promise

The show exists to make the whole range economically and socially real: AI builder, musician, host, comic, writer, interviewer, teacher, street philosopher, and operator. The show gives the depth a public entry point so people are not hit with the whole cathedral at once.

Phase -1: Pre-flight and Rule Recon

Phase -1 happens before any meaningful public setup. The point is to create enough structure that the first public tests feel real without becoming heavy.

Goals

Reserve and configure the public surfaces: marcusvale.tv, The Marcus Vale Variety Show accounts, YouTube, Facebook, Instagram.

Prepare a tiny kit that supports play but does not create a production footprint.

Clarify the Venice rules enough to avoid avoidable friction.

Create a simple field note system so every session produces learning, even if no footage is published.

Phase -1 kit

One small whiteboard or cardboard sign: The Marcus Vale Variety Show.

One daily menu marker: Today's Menu.

One or two toys max: guitar, drum pad, sketch pad, question cards, AI demo laptop, or tiny instrument.

One phone for photos and notes.

Optional audio recorder for personal review only.

Hand sanitizer, water, sunscreen, battery pack, and a packable layer.

Questions to ask Venice Beach Recreation Center

Where can a solo performer / interviewer / artist set up on Ocean Front Walk?

Do I need a Public Expression Permit if I am not selling anything?

Can I accept donations? If yes, under what conditions?

Can I use a small sign, one stool/table, one camera, and one microphone?

Can I use a tripod? Can it be on the ground or only inside designated spaces?

Can I use any amplified sound? If not, are small acoustic instruments allowed?

If I film direct participants for YouTube/social media, when does that require a FilmLA permit?

Are there walk-on spaces after noon, and what happens if the permit holder arrives later?

What are the current enforcement priorities since the recent crackdown?

Who should I talk to before a larger recurring activation?

Phase 0: The Unfilmed Ritual

Phase 0 is the soul test. The show starts as street art, not content. This phase proves whether strangers will stop and co-create before cameras, algorithms, and production value start distorting the field.

Setup

Small sign or board: The Marcus Vale Variety Show.

Daily menu with one or two invitations, not ten.

No lavs. No full camera table. No donation jar at first.

One toy or prompt per session: free drum lesson, make a song, ask me anything about AI, paint something, street philosophy question, five-minute band, or build your idea.

Short test windows: 60 to 90 minutes.

Pack immediately if asked to move.

Sample menus

Today's Menu: Free drum lesson + one question about AI.

Today's Menu: Make a tiny song + street philosophy.

Today's Menu: Build your idea with AI for five minutes.

Today's Menu: Paint something + tell me one true thing.

Today's Menu: Let's make you a band.

Phase 0 script

"I'm testing a public variety show. Want to make something for five minutes? No pressure. It can be music, art, AI, a question, whatever."

Success metrics

At least 3 people stop or ask what it is.

At least 1 person participates for more than 2 minutes.

Marcus feels alive rather than performative.

People leave warmer than they arrived.

At least one repeatable menu idea emerges.

Phase 1: Ambient Documentation

Phase 1 adds a camera, but the camera is not the point. The goal is to document the field enough for private review and maybe light public clips, without turning the interaction into a shoot.

Setup

One wide camera off to the side: phone, Zoom handy camera, or DJI on a small tripod.

No lavs unless the participant explicitly wants to be recorded clearly.

No table-based camera station yet.

One visible sign that filming may be happening if the camera is recording public activity.

Consent before using any direct participant as a featured clip.

Short sessions, ideally 60 to 120 minutes.

Purpose of the footage

Review body language, approachability, crowd flow, and menu effectiveness.

Find accidental moments that reveal the show's real shape.

Train the eye before building the full camera setup.

Publish only what feels dignified and consent-safe.

Phase 2: Participant-Aware Micro-stage

Phase 2 is the first true content-ready version. It uses hands-free cameras because the show requires Marcus to be present, not holding a camera. This phase should be small enough to move quickly and serious enough to produce usable footage.

Micro-stage kit

One small table or stool.

Two DJI Osmo Pocket-style cameras on tabletop tripods: one on Marcus, one on the guest.

Two lav mics or a small recorder setup.

One tiny wide shot camera if feasible.

One handwritten sign and a daily menu board.

No amplified sound in early tests unless explicitly permitted.

No donation jar until the Public Expression / vending rules are clarified.

No cables crossing pedestrian flow.

No blocking the Boardwalk or creating crowd hazards.

Consent flow

Before filming directly: "I'm recording this for a public variety-show project. Are you cool being on camera?"

For deeper segments: get a simple release or at least a recorded verbal consent.

For minors: do not feature them without parent/guardian consent.

For visibly impaired, intoxicated, distressed, or unstable participants: do not make them content. Offer ordinary human kindness off-camera if appropriate.

If someone asks not to be shown, respect it immediately.

Phase 2 test rules

Test after noon in an available public-expression area if using a Boardwalk space.

Keep the first micro-stage tests to 60 to 90 minutes.

Move instantly if staff, rangers, LAPD, or a permit holder asks you to move.

Treat enforcement as data, not humiliation.

Do not argue in the field. Ask: "Where should I do this properly?"

Phase 3: Permitted Production and Recurring Show

Phase 3 begins only after the format has real signal. At this point, MVS is no longer just a street experiment. It becomes a recurring public media property, and the operating model should become professional.

Triggers to enter Phase 3

At least 10 public sessions completed.

At least 20 usable clips or moments collected.

A clear menu format that strangers understand quickly.

A repeating location pattern that works without hostility.

Audience or participant demand for more.

Need for larger gear, recurring schedule, live stream, crew, or amplified sound.

Phase 3 production package

Permit path clarified with Venice Beach Recreation Center and FilmLA.

Insurance plan identified before permit application.

Portable rig with clean cable management and no obstruction.

Crew roles defined: host, camera/audio, field producer, safety/gear watch, editor.

Consent/release process ready.

Content calendar and post-session edit flow ready.

Emergency/weather/gear-loss plan ready.

Permit and Rule Guide

This section is not legal advice. It summarizes the practical rule landscape so the show can stay confident without being sloppy.

Venice Boardwalk public-expression rules

The Boardwalk / Ocean Front Walk has designated public-expression spaces regulated by L.A.M.C. Section 42.15.

The ordinance defines 205 Designated Spaces on the Boardwalk between Navy Street and 17th Avenue.

The rules distinguish between expressive activity, performance, vending, and prohibited commercial retail activity.

Original art, books, music, political/philosophical/ideological material, and performance are different from buying wholesale goods and flipping them.

The city regulates tables, equipment, access areas, umbrellas, generators, cables, obstruction, and post-sunset activity.

The practical rule is simple: equipment and stationary setups put the activity closer to the regulated Boardwalk space system.

Public Expression Permit path

The in-person hub is Venice Beach Recreation Center, 1800 Ocean Front Walk, Venice, CA 90291.

Official recreation center hours listed by LA Parks: Monday to Friday 8 am to 6 pm, Saturday 8 am to 4 pm, closed Sundays and city holidays.

Ask specifically about Public Expression permits, P-Zone / I-Zone spaces, walk-on rules, and current enforcement practices.

Do not rely only on vendor folklore. The ground culture and the official rules may diverge.

FilmLA path

LA Parks says commercial productions, motion picture, video taping, and still photography for TV or public exhibition at Venice Beach require a permit from the Department's Park Film Office / FilmLA process.

LA Parks says Venice filming/photography permits are generally Monday through Friday, no weekends or holidays, and payments must be made at least 2 days prior to the shoot date.

FilmLA says permit applicants must provide proof of liability insurance before FilmLA can coordinate and release permits, and workers' compensation is required by California law, even for productions using 1099 workers.

FilmLA's 2026 Low Impact Permit Pilot is available in the City of Los Angeles for qualifying low-impact shoots and offers lower fees, but it still has limits and still requires insurance.

The Low Impact Knowledge Base describes limits including City of LA only, up to 3 locations over 3 consecutive days for qualifying productions, and a minimum of 3 full business days for coordination.

Note: The key distinction is not "camera equals permit" in every practical moment. The distinction is whether the setup looks like casual field documentation or a public production with equipment, crowd impact, commercial/public exhibition intent, or repeated use of a public location.

Field Operating Rules

Start smaller than the dream. The first proof is aliveness.

Never block pedestrian flow or emergency access.

Do not sell commercial retail goods. If selling anything, keep it expressive and verify the permit path.

No poverty content. Help off-camera unless someone explicitly wants to participate.

Consent before direct filming. Consent again before publishing anything sensitive.

If asked to move, move immediately and respectfully.

No arguing with rangers, LAPD, permit holders, or local regulars in the field.

Keep the gear packable enough to leave in under five minutes.

Do not create a crowd before crowd control is figured out.

Keep the show sober-governed. High-energy does not mean chaotic.

Recommended First 30 Days in LA

Week 1: walk Venice at different times. Map crowd flow, performers, boardwalk spaces, Rec Center, rangers, local regulars, and quiet edges.

Week 1: visit Venice Beach Recreation Center and ask the rule questions in person. Do not rely on hearsay.

Week 1: run one Phase 0 unfilmed ritual with one small sign and one toy.

Week 2: run two more Phase 0 sessions. Change only one variable each time: menu, location, or prop.

Week 2: add one off-side camera for private review only if the ritual feels alive.

Week 3: publish one low-stakes proof post on marcusvale.tv or the main Marcus account, if the footage is dignified and consent-safe.

Week 3: test the first V0.5 micro-stage with two tiny cameras only if previous sessions prove people will stop without pressure.

Week 4: decide whether to enter the Public Expression Permit path or keep the format mobile longer.

Week 4: if the show has real signal, call FilmLA or email FilmLA Solutions Services to ask whether the planned setup fits Low Impact or requires Standard permitting.
